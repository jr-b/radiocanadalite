Hamilton high school flying flag at half-mast after drive-by shooting kills student

Bobby Hristova | CBC News | Posted: Monday, September 27, 2021, 5:44 PM UTC | Last Updated: 22 hours ago

There are no suspect descriptions yet, according to police

Image | Miscellaneous

Caption: Bernie Custis Secondary School is lowering its flag to mourn the death of a student. (Christine Rankin)

Bernie Custis Secondary School (BCSS) will fly its flag at half-mast to mourn the death of an 18-year-old student killed late last week.

"Students and staff at Bernie Custis Secondary School are saddened to learn of the death of 18-year-old student, Kaw Kaw. We all share in the grief and sadness of having lost a student in our Hamilton community," read a letter from principal Sonya Sykes to families.

"We will continue to support the school community and reach out to our community partners."

Ward 3 Trustee Maria Felix Miller also expressed condolences online.

"Social workers are available at BCSS for support to any student who may require help or support during this difficult time," she tweeted.

"We are also supporting students and staff at Prince of Wales and Cathy Wever elementary schools. Staff are being supported through our Employee Supports and Wellness team. Please reach out if you need to."

Embed | Miscellaneous

Police said on Friday they responded to reports of a shooting near Cannon Street East and Barnesdale Avenue North at roughly 4:35 p.m.

Police said Yua Blut Kaw was walking in the Stipley neighbourhood when multiple shots were fired from a dark-coloured, four-door sedan.

He was taken to hospital and pronounced dead, according to police. There are no suspect descriptions yet.

It is the city's 15th homicide this year.

Image | Frank Bergen

Caption: Hamilton Police Service Chief Frank Bergen is shown during a ceremony outside the central police station on Sept. 27, 2021. (Dan Taekema/CBC)

Speaking broadly on gun crime in the city, Chief Frank Bergen told media on Monday it is hard to pinpoint the cause.

"It would be easy if we could actually say it was organized crime. What makes it [more] difficult is the fact that many of these interactions are disorganized crime," he said. "It's people that believe they need to protect their small corner of their turf and the reality is what they're doing is going after each other in retaliation for control over the drug market or gun market."
