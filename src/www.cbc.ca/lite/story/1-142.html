As a 45-year-old farmer recovers from COVID-19, his wife and friends run 90 kilometres

Samantha Craggs | CBC News | Posted: Thursday, September 23, 2021, 10:23 PM UTC | Last Updated: September 23

Sarah VanNetten and a team of 9 are doing a relay from Simcoe to Hamilton

Image | Miscellaneous

Caption: Sarah VanNetten, with husband Mike, is doing a 90-kilometre relay run with friends to raise money for Hamilton Health Sciences after he nearly died from COVID-19. (Sarah VanNetten)

Earlier this year, Sarah VanNetten drove from Simcoe, Ont., to Hamilton every day to see her husband Mike, a hospitalized COVID-19 patient clinging to his life on a machine that did the work of his heart and lungs. 

But on Friday, she and her team are travelling the route another way — on foot.

From April to June, Sarah spent each day gowned and masked at Hamilton General Hospital, sitting at her 45-year-old husband's bedside as an extracorporeal membrane oxygenation (ECMO) machine kept him alive. The nurses knew Sarah by name. When Mike was in a coma, they held one-sided sports conversations with him. Every little sign of progress — a lift, a step, a walk — the workers gave Mike a round of applause.

Now that Mike — a farmer and factory worker known around town by his nickname, Chicken — is home and recovering, Sarah is doing a 90-kilometre Simcoe-to-Hamilton relay marathon as part of Hamilton Health Sciences Foundation's Strides for Health-Care Heroes. The foundation is fundraising for a new ECMO machine.

Sarah and the team, called Chicken's HIIT Chicks, will start at the VanNetten farm. They'll use trails to walk and ride the route, switching off five kilometres at a time. They've already raised more than $17,000.

Sarah says it's the least she can do. 

Image | Mike VanNetten

Caption: Mike VanNetten stands with his wife as he leaves Hamilton General Hospital on June 30. (Submitted by Hamilton Health Sciences)

"They are the kindest, most intelligent people," she said of the health-care workers. "They saved Mike's life."

The VanNetten family's COVID-19 journey started in the spring. 

On April 3, the father of four had symptoms that resembled a bad cold — cough, chills, inability to sleep. On April 8, he went to Norfolk General Hospital, and within minutes, he was given oxygen.

Team Chicken

He spent weeks in critical condition at Hamilton General Hospital, and word of his illness spread.

Norfolk County residents started a campaign called Lights On for Chicken, keeping their porch lights on and putting out hockey sticks. A local restaurant raised money for the family. Sarah posted daily Facebook updates, which drew hundreds of comments. She just published Steady As We Go: COVID, Chicken and a Miracle.

This week, a group of Mike's friends, dubbed Team Chicken, raised another $17,000 for a five-kilometre walk from the Simcoe Arms pub to the local golf course and back again.

The foundation hopes to raise $100,000 this year, said spokesperson Aaron Lam. In addition to an ECMO machine, it wants to buy a Spirit bed with enhanced features for patients at St. Peter's Hospital.

ECMO machines weren't made to treat COVID-19 patients, Lam said. But "it has been found very effective for treating patients who are really strongly affected by the COVID-19 virus."

'You're not promised tomorrow'

"In Mike VanNetten's case, his lungs were so damaged by the virus that basically his lungs couldn't take in enough oxygen to pump enough blood to oxygenate his body," Lam said.

This is the second year the foundation has done a virtual run. People can donate until Sept. 26.

As for Mike, "he's doing amazingly well from where he came from," Sarah said. "His biggest struggles are energy level and rebuilding strength. He's not sleeping great. But overall, we're just so grateful and so thankful."

The main lesson, she said, has been that "you can't take a day for granted.

"Every day is a gift and you're not promised tomorrow. So every day, do what you can."
