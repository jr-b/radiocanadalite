Drenching rain broke 1-day records for Hamilton

Dan Taekema | CBC News | Posted: Thursday, September 23, 2021, 5:35 PM UTC | Last Updated: September 23

Airport weather station has measured 175.4 millimetres of rain so far this month

Image | Miscellaneous

Caption: Hamilton is drying out after a heavy rainfall that pounded the city over the past two days. (Adam Carter/CBC)

The day-long drenching that soaked Hamilton on Wednesday also set daily rainfall records for the city.

A total of 46.2 millimetres of rain were measured at the airport weather station, draining the previous one-day record for Sept. 22, which was 44.5 millimetres and set in 1989, according to Gerald Chen, a meteorologist with Environment Canada.

The precipitation captured at the Royal Botanical Gardens' station also topped its previous Sept. 22 high-water mark of 27.4 millimetres, set in 2000. A whopping 51.9 millimetres were collected Wednesday, said Chen.

But despite the deluge, Hamilton hasn't broken its monthly record of 206.1 millimetres, set in September 1977.

In a normal September, the airport sees 82 millimetres, said Chen. So far, including Wednesday, it has hit 175.4 millimetres.

For now, this month will have to settle for being the second rainiest September on record.

"We [would] really need another big system to go through the area," said Chen, adding the region may see some showers in the next few days.

"It's not impossible, but we really need some help. I don't know if people actually want this record to be broken," the meteorologist added with a laugh.

Flood watch still in place

Rainfall amounts of 30 to 77 millimetres were measured across the city on Wednesday, with Stoney Creek getting hit especially hard, said Jonathan Bastien, water resources engineer with the Hamilton Conservation Authority (HCA).

While some creek levels are high, no significant flooding has been observed.

In some cases, the full impact of a heavy rainfall won't be felt until a day or two later, so a flood watch remains in place. HCA staff will continue to monitor levels and is asking people to exercise caution around the slippery banks of waterways.

"So far so good," said Bastien. "There's nothing really concerning."

As for the rest of fall, Chen said the long-term weather models don't offer many clues of what to expect.

"It's a toss-up. I would advise people to be prepared to anything," he said. "Fall and winter are the season that keep on giving with systems that bring rain, wind and snow."
